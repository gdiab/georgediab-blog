One of my engineers said something to me a few weeks ago that I've been thinking about ever since.

She'd been quiet for months. Not disengaged, just... processing. She's been writing code for over a decade, the kind of engineer who could hold an entire system architecture in her head and then just build it. Watching AI tools start doing chunks of that work, fluently and fast, was clearly doing something to her. I didn't push.

Then one day she came by and said: "I do have good product sense and good taste. Maybe I'll be ok."

I didn't say anything for a second. Because that's not a small thing. That's not someone making peace with less. That's someone who found the other side.

## The Grief Is Real

Here's what I think gets missed in most of the discourse around AI and engineers: the craft wasn't just a skill. It was an identity.

Programmers don't just write code. They belong to something. There's a culture, a community, a shared language, a set of values around how good software gets made. Dave Gauer (ratfactor.com), writing about what he calls "heirloom programmers," put it well: "A social identity isn't about typing on a keyboard. It's about belonging to a group." When the thing that granted you membership in that group starts getting automated, the loss isn't just economic. It's existential.

I saw a thread on Reddit recently where someone wrote: "The skill that mattered most was being the bottleneck between an idea and reality. That bottleneck is dissolving." That line hit me because it's precise. The thing engineers were uniquely capable of, the thing that made them indispensable, was being the only ones who could translate intention into working software. AI is eroding that exclusivity fast.

And this isn't just a programmer problem. Operations, legal, finance, anyone whose professional identity was built on mastery of a complex, learnable craft is going through some version of this. Engineers are just further along on the timeline.

## Your Team Is Somewhere on This Map

Kübler-Ross's five stages were about death, but they describe identity loss too. And if you manage a team right now, you're watching all five stages play out in real time, often in the same sprint.

The denial shows up as dismissal: "AI-generated code is garbage, I'd never use it." The anger shows up as resentment toward colleagues who do use it, or toward leadership for encouraging it. Bargaining sounds like: "OK, I'll use it for boilerplate, but the real architecture is still mine." Depression is the quiet withdrawal, the engineer who used to be the most engaged person in the room and now just ships tickets without much commentary.

And acceptance, when it comes, looks like my engineer. Not defeated. Reoriented.

Psychology Today has written about how "conventional identity," the kind built on measurable skill, title, and where you sit in a hierarchy, is inherently fragile because external forces can strip it away. That's not a new insight, but it lands differently when the external force is a model running on a GPU somewhere that can do in ten minutes what took you a week to learn to do. This isn't just economic disruption. It's identity dismantling.

As a manager, your job at this stage isn't to talk people out of their grief. Recognize where each person is. Don't rush them. Someone in the anger stage doesn't need a pep talk about AI productivity, they need to feel heard. Someone in depression might need a 1:1 that has nothing to do with tickets.

## The Fracture Inside Teams

What makes this harder is that teams don't move through this together. You end up with people at completely different stages trying to build the same thing.

Gauer's "heirloom programmers" are the ones who feel the loss of craft most acutely. Simon Højberg described it sharply: "Creative puzzle-solving is left to the machines, and we become mere operators disassociated from our craft." For engineers who found deep meaning in the problem-solving itself, that's not a small thing to grieve.

On the other end, you've got engineers who have leaned in completely, shipping twice as fast, treating AI as a multiplier. They're not wrong. They're also sometimes tone-deaf to what their colleagues are going through.

I follow a Substack called Operations Optimist, written by someone who isn't a programmer at all, an ops professional who spent years mastering document preparation and financial modeling. AI does that work in an afternoon now. She describes herself as sitting "between bargaining and acceptance." Her experience is a useful mirror for engineers: this isn't about whether you can code. It's about what happens when the thing you built your professional self around gets automated. Everyone's dealing with some version of it. The programmer's version is just more visible right now.

The tension this creates inside a team is real and it's worth naming. People at different stages, with different identities, trying to collaborate and build something together. That friction doesn't disappear on its own.

## What Comes Out the Other Side

Back to my engineer.

"I have good product sense and good taste."

That's a post-conventional identity. Psychology Today's framework distinguishes between conventional identity (role, title, measurable skill) and post-conventional identity (judgment, systems thinking, values). The latter is harder to automate. Not impossible, but substantially harder.

The new moat for engineers isn't code authorship. It's knowing what to build, why it matters, and whether what's being built is actually good. That's taste. It's the thing that separates an engineer who can ship features from one who can tell you which features are worth shipping at all.

The engineers who are going to thrive in this next phase aren't necessarily the ones who write the most code. They're the ones who know what the right code looks like. Who can look at a system and say "this is wrong" before it ships. Who can talk to a product manager and translate fuzzy intent into something buildable and valuable. Who can review AI-generated output and catch the places where it's technically correct but wrong.

Taste. Judgment. Systems thinking. Those aren't soft skills tacked onto engineering. They're the core skill set in a world where the mechanical parts are increasingly handled for you.

## What You Can Do as a Manager

Create space for the grief. This might be the most important one. If your team senses that the official party line is "embrace AI and move fast," the people struggling will go underground. They'll perform acceptance while privately falling apart. That's bad for them and bad for your team's actual output.

Watch for people stuck in bargaining or depression. The transition has a real cost, and it's not evenly distributed. Some people move through quickly. Others get stuck. An engineer stuck in depression can look like low engagement, missed deadlines, or quiet disengagement from code reviews. It's worth a direct conversation.

Name the new value explicitly. If you only celebrate shipping velocity and lines of code, that's what your team will optimize for. Start celebrating taste. When someone catches a bad design decision early, name it. When someone pushes back on a feature direction because they have a better read on user behavior, name that too. The things that matter now need to be visible.

Your job isn't to convince anyone that AI is good. It's to help your team find their footing on the other side of a real disruption.

---

That engineer who told me she has good product sense and good taste? She's going to be more than ok. She figured out the thing that takes most people a long time to find: the skill AI can't replace is the one that decides what's worth building in the first place.

The identity crisis is real. The resolution is too. It just takes time to get there, and it helps to have a manager who knows the difference between someone who's stuck and someone who's still on their way.
